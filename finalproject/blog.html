Boston Bruins center Patrice Bergeron redefines meaning of playoff warrior in Stanley Cup Final

By Amanda Bruno | June 23, 2013, 5:11 PM EST

Boston Bruins center Patrice Bergeron really wanted to win the Stanley Cup.

No, he really, really wanted it.

The assistant captain went to great lengths, doing whatever possible to be out on the ice with his teammates.

You might as well rephrase the term playoff warrior to Patrice Bergeron.

After the Bruins were shocked by Chicago in Game 6 of the Stanley Cup Final Monday night at TD Garden, as the Blackhawks captured the best-of-7 series, Bergeron made himself available in the locker room and revealed his ailments.

He said he played with a broken rib, torn cartilage and a separated shoulder.

It wasn’t known when these injuries were exactly sustained.

During the team’s breakup day at the Garden Wednesday, general manager Peter Chiarelli shed more light on Bergeron's health during a press conference.

Chiarelli said to deal with the torn cartilage, Bergeron opted to have a freezing procedure done to block a nerve in order to play Game 6.

Postgame Bergeron suffered a small puncture in his lung from either the nerve blockage or the cracked rib.

If the puncture happened during the game, Bergeron would not have been able to play.

He went straight to the hospital for observation after meeting with the media and is still currently being treated.

With that all said Chiarelli insists Bergeron is fine.

His shoulder separated during the first period, but he did not miss a shift and finished with 17:45 of ice time — his lowest total of the series.

That is just crazy.

Last postseason, Bergeron played with a torn oblique muscle. He suffered it in Game 3 of the first round against the Washington Capitals. He aggravated it in Game 5 and on top of that got his nose broken by an accidental elbow by Alexander Semin.

The pain was obvious.

Bergeron took only one draw in Game 7, which he won, and wasn't able to stretch far enough to potentially score the game-winning goal in overtime before Joel Ward did as the Capitals knocked off the defending Stanley Cup champions.

Learning the specifics of what Bergeron was ailing from this postseason is mind boggling.

This guy refused to sit out.

He could have been hit by a bus and still be in the lineup the next game.

Never in a million years would you hear this kind of story about a Major League Baseball player, which has been the running joke.

The Bruins are lucky to have Bergeron. Hopefully he is going to be OK.

FAREWELL FERENCE: Chiarelli also revealed at his press conference that he told defenseman Andrew Ference that the club will not be resigning him.

It was a tough conversation to have, Chiarelli said.

The 34-year-old is scheduled to be an unrestricted free agent on July 1 with his three-year, $6.75 million contract expiring.

With the salary cap dropping from $70.2 million to $64.3 million next season, the Bruins simply can't afford to keep everyone. And with the emergence of rookie defensemen Torey Krug and Matt Bartkowski during the playoffs, it makes the decision to let Ference go a little easier.

Ference means a lot to the organization. His locker room presence and leadership will be missed.

His Player of the Game tradition, which started during the 2011 playoffs with the vintage Bruins Starter jacket, shows his true character.

He carried on the tradition with The Chain in 2012.

But mostly notably, his mark will be left with the Army ranger jacket who his friend, active army ranger at the time Lucas Carr gave to him after a visit to the Fort Benning base in Georgia.

After last night's 5-2 embarrassment against a Philadelphia Flyers club who is missing a number of key players and will be hanging up their skates for the season after two games, the confidence meter has dropped to a five.

That might even be a bit generous.

Tuesday night revealed the sad truth of the current state of this Bruins team.

The desperation wasn't there. The physicality was missing in action. The urgency, nonexistent.

The amount of odd-man rushes, giveaways, and turnovers was ridiculous.

And the mental mistakes? Unbearable.

Captain Zdeno Chara couldn't handle a simple dump in play. Instead of passing the puck back behind his own goal, he threw it towards a surprised Anton Khudobin who let it slip by. Oliver Lauridsen was credited with the score as the Flyers took a 3-1 lead at 11:31 in the second period.

That came only seven seconds after right winger Matt Read showed off superb hand-eye coordination by batting the puck in midair with his stick to give the Flyers a 2-1 edge.

Just like that, Khudobin was pulled and replaced by Tuukka Rask who fared no better. Rask allowed two goals in the third, including one only 13 seconds after Bruins center David Krejci scored to make it, 4-2 at 8:20.

Khudobin was brought back in and no further damage was made.

As dismal as the goaltending was for Boston, coach Claude Julien said Khudobin was not responsible for the loss.

Poor puck management, poor decision making, and wake-up call were popular phrases.

The last time the Bruins allowed five goals in regulation was in a 6-5 shootout loss to the Montreal Canadiens on March 27.

It should have been the other way around.

There's been a lot of talk about if the Bruins can simply flip the switch once the playoffs start.

Don't buy into it.

Bruins general manager Peter Chiarelli confirmed the notion on 98.5 The Sports Hub's Toucher and Rich show Wednesday saying, it just doesn't happen.

Just look at last season where everyone thought the Bruins would just magically show up against the Washington Capitals in the Eastern Conference quarterfinals.

Instead Capitals netminder Braden Holtby, who we knew little about, helped eliminate Boston in seven games as it couldn't create dirty, gritty goals on top of scoring once in four games.

Every shot the B's took was right at Holtby and that kind of play, plus lousy decisions, isn't going to win a playoff series.

Granted the Bruins were without defenseman Adam McQuaid and wing Nathan Horton (concussions), right winger Tyler Seguin played with a detached tendon in his left knuckle, Chara suffered a broken nose, and center Patrice Bergeron was playing with a torn oblique muscle and broken nose.

So what's the excuse now?

Maybe the Bruins will prove everyone wrong, but a lot needs to happen from now to Sunday as the regular season closes.
